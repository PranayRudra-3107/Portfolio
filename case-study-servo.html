Servo — Service Providing Mobile App

Published · ICAISS 2023 · Conference paper

01 — Problem

Finding reliable local services is still broken

In India, the informal service economy is vast — electricians, plumbers, tutors, cleaners — but discovery is fragmented across WhatsApp groups, word-of-mouth, and unreliable directories. Trust between client and provider is a fundamental barrier to the first booking.

Existing platforms either targeted premium urban markets (UrbanClap / Urban Company) or were too generic. The gap was a trust-first, mobile-first experience built for Tier-2 and Tier-3 city users.

The core design challenge wasn't the booking flow — it was building enough trust for a stranger to invite a service provider into their home on the first interaction.

02 — Research

Dual-sided research: users and providers

Servo is a two-sided marketplace. I ran separate research tracks for each side:

Clients (service seekers):

  • 8 interviews across age groups 22–55 in Hyderabad
  • Key anxieties: overcharging, reliability, safety, no-shows
  • Mental model: "I want to see who they are before I confirm"

Service providers:

  • 5 interviews with electricians, plumbers, and tutors
  • Key barriers: irregular income, no ability to showcase skills or reviews, difficulty managing bookings
  • Mental model: "If I do good work, I want the next customer to know that"
Dual journey map — client vs. provider: divergent needs, shared trust gap
03 — Define

The trust system is the product

From both research tracks, a unified theme emerged: the core UX challenge was designing a trust system, not just a booking flow.

Design principles:

  • Identity before action — provider profiles must establish credibility before a user sees pricing
  • Verified signals only — review counts, verification badges, and response rate visible on the browse screen
  • Provider agency — providers can set availability, decline requests, and build a portfolio
  • Lightweight onboarding — registration under 3 minutes for both sides, with progressive profile completion
04 — Design

Designing for both sides of the marketplace

The app comprises two distinct experiences within one product: the client-facing discovery/booking flow and the provider-facing availability/job management flow.

Client experience:

  • Category browse → provider cards (photo, rating, verified badge, distance, hourly rate)
  • Provider profile with portfolio images, verified reviews, and response time
  • Booking flow: select service → pick time → confirm with estimated cost
  • In-app chat with read receipts for pre-job communication

Provider experience:

  • Job inbox with accept/decline/counter-propose
  • Availability calendar with block-off functionality
  • Earnings dashboard with job history
  • Profile builder with skill tags and portfolio upload
High-fidelity screens — client browse, provider profile, booking confirmation

Design choices were grounded in the trust-first principle: the provider's photo, name, and review count appear before any pricing information. Price is the last thing shown, after trust signals are established.

05 — Testing

Usability testing and the published findings

I ran moderated usability testing with 10 participants (5 clients, 5 providers) using a Figma prototype:

  • Overall task completion rate: 85%
  • Client booking flow completion: 90%
  • Provider job-acceptance flow: 80%
  • SUS (System Usability Scale) score: 76 — "Good"

Key iteration from testing: providers initially didn't trust that accepting a job was binding. Adding a "confirmation screen" with job details summary before final acceptance resolved this.

The research methodology, findings, and design outcomes were written up and accepted at ICAISS 2023 — the International Conference on Artificial Intelligence in Smart Systems.

06 — Outcome

Results and reflection

  • Published at ICAISS 2023 — formal academic validation of the research and design approach
  • 85% task completion across both user types in usability testing
  • The dual-sided research framework became a template I've used in subsequent projects

Designing a two-sided marketplace taught me that the product you're designing is the relationship between the two sides — not just the interface each side sees.

This was my first end-to-end UX project — from research brief to published paper. It established the research-first approach I've carried into every project since.
