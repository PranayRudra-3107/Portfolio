Agentic RAG — Conversational Research Interface

Figma mockup · High-fidelity prototype

01 — Problem

The problem with AI research tools

Teams using RAG-based AI systems kept hitting the same wall: results arrived without context. Users couldn't tell why the system retrieved a particular chunk, whether it had checked multiple sources, or how confident the answer actually was.

The underlying LangGraph pipeline was sophisticated — implementing Self-RAG, Corrective RAG, and Adaptive RAG patterns — but the interface treated it like a simple chatbot. Users either over-trusted or completely distrusted the outputs.

"I never know if it's making things up or if it actually searched the document. So I re-read everything anyway."
— Researcher during discovery interview

02 — Research

Understanding how people reason with AI

I conducted 6 contextual interviews with researchers and analysts who regularly used AI-assisted document review tools. Key themes emerged:

  • Provenance anxiety — users wanted to see the source, not just the answer
  • Process opacity — multi-step agent reasoning was invisible and unsettling
  • Calibration failure — confidence scores were shown numerically but not acted on behaviourally
  • Recovery cost — when the system was wrong, finding the correct answer manually was slow

I also ran a competitive analysis of Perplexity AI, Notion AI, and You.com to benchmark citation patterns and confidence communication.

Affinity map — 6 user interviews · 38 coded observations · 4 themes
03 — Define

Design goals

From synthesis, I defined three non-negotiable design goals:

  • Make the agent's reasoning visible — show the retrieval steps, not just the final answer
  • Ground every claim in a source — inline citations with hover-preview of original text
  • Give users control over depth — progressive disclosure: summary first, detail on demand

How might we help users calibrate their trust in AI-generated answers without adding cognitive overhead?

04 — Design

From sketches to high-fidelity

I ran two rounds of wireframing. The first explored three distinct layout patterns: a linear chat, a split-pane document viewer, and a "research journal" with expandable reasoning nodes. User walkthroughs favoured the split-pane for complex queries, but the chat for quick lookups.

The final design uses an adaptive layout: single-pane chat by default, expanding to a two-column document view when citations are surfaced.

Wireframe iterations — Round 1: 3 concepts · Round 2: refined split-pane layout

Key interface decisions:

  • Reasoning timeline — a collapsible "steps" panel showing which RAG strategy fired (retrieve → grade → re-query → generate)
  • Inline citations — [¹] markers that expand to the source chunk on hover, no modal interruptions
  • Confidence indicator — a colour-coded bar (green / amber / red) based on retrieval quality score, not raw model confidence
  • Correction pathway — a "Refine this answer" affordance that re-triggers the corrective RAG loop with user guidance
High-fidelity prototype — Chat view with reasoning timeline expanded
05 — Testing

Usability testing

I ran moderated think-aloud sessions with 4 participants using a Figma prototype. Tasks included: find a specific regulation clause, compare two source documents, and identify a hallucinated answer.

  • 100% task completion on citation-lookup tasks (vs 60% baseline with original interface)
  • Hallucination detection improved: 3 of 4 users correctly identified the injected incorrect answer
  • The reasoning timeline was opened in 7 of 8 complex-query sessions — higher than expected

One critical finding: users didn't understand the confidence bar without a label. Iteration added a plain-English descriptor ("High confidence · 4 sources verified").

06 — Outcome

Impact

After implementing the redesign alongside the LangGraph pipeline:

  • Time-to-answer (task completion) dropped by ~40% in follow-up sessions
  • Hallucination detection rate increased to 75% from 40% with the old interface
  • The evaluation scripts I designed to measure hallucination rate and retrieval quality enabled 3× faster design iterations

The biggest lesson: AI interface design is fundamentally about calibrating trust, not just displaying information. The visual design is the confidence infrastructure.
